TRICK OR TREAT


Living with mental illness is not easy, but treatment can help improve the sufferer's quality of life. Try seeing a medical professional to ask for help with treatment to get better. If it is still relatively mild, self-care may help. If self-treatment is no longer effective, the sufferer may need psychiatric counseling or prescription medication from a doctor. Several methods that can be an option for overcoming or treating depression include:
  1. Self-care
    Self-care can be the best option for treating mild depression. For many people, regular exercise helps create positive feelings and improves mood. Getting enough quality sleep, eating healthy foods and avoiding alcohol (depressants) can also help reduce symptoms.
  2. Psychotherapy
    Doctors will recommend psychotherapy for cases of mild to severe depression. Apart from that, doctors also often combine psychotherapy with medication. The following types of therapy to treat these mental disorders:
    • Cognitive behavior therapy (CBT). This therapy aims to help sufferers release negative thoughts and feelings and replace them with positive responses.
    • Problem-solving therapy (PST), therapy that can help improve the sufferer's ability to deal with experiences that trigger feelings of depression.
    • Interpersonal therapy (IPT), to help overcome problems that arise when relating to other people.
    • Psychodynamic therapy, to help sufferers understand what they are feeling and how to respond to these feelings.

    Depending on the severity of the condition, treatment may take several weeks or longer. In many cases, significant improvement can take place within 10 to 15 sessions.

  3. Medication
    A chemical imbalance in the brain is a risk factor for depression. Doctors can prescribe antidepressants to help modify a person's brain chemistry. Generally, antidepressant drugs do not have a stimulating effect in people who do not have the disorder. Antidepressants may reduce symptoms within the first week or two of consumption, but full benefits may not be seen for two to three months. In some situations, other psychotropic medications may be helpful. Doctors usually recommend that sufferers continue taking medication for six months or more after their symptoms improve. Long-term treatment can also help to reduce the risk of future depressive episodes for people who are more at risk. Several classes of antidepressant drugs that are widely used are:
    • Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs): fluoxetine and sertraline
    • Serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs): duloxetine and venlafaxine
    • Monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOI): phenelzined and tranylcypromine
    • Tetracyclic antidepressants: mirtazapine and maprotiline
    • Tricyclic antidepressants (TCA): amitriptyline, amoxapine, and imipramine
    • Dopaminergic norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (DNRI): bupropion.

    The recommended antidepressant drugs are second generation antidepressant drugs. Examples of drugs are fluoxetine, duloxetine, escitalopram, sertraline, venlafaxine, trazodone, bupropion, and mirtazapine. When consuming this medicine, it must be accompanied by a doctor's prescription.

  4. Brain stimulation therapy
    This type of therapy is usually an option for depression conditions that do not improve after taking medication, experiencing symptoms of psychosis, and showing attempts to commit suicide. This type of brain stimulation therapy includes:
    • Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT). This therapy is carried out by sending an electric current to the brain through the scalp to cause a short seizure. ECT therapy is only used for people with severe depression.
    • Transcranial Magnetic Stimulation (TMS). This type of brain stimulation takes place using magnetic energy which is converted into an electric current at the bottom of the skull. This procedure aims to help sufferers regulate emotions. TMS is an additional treatment that can be combined with medication and is non-invasive (does not require surgery).
    • Vagus Nerve Stimulation (VHS). Doctors rarely perform this type of therapy. The procedure is that the doctor will install electrodes to stimulate the vagus nerve which are implanted in the sufferer's neck.
